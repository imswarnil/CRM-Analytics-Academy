# CRM Basics

Before analytics, there is the **CRM** — the system of record that holds your customer relationships. This lesson assumes zero prior knowledge and walks the story in chronological order, so the terms we use for the rest of the course feel natural.

## What "CRM" actually means

**CRM** stands for **Customer Relationship Management**. At its simplest, it is the practice — and the software — of tracking every interaction a business has with its customers and prospects in one place.

A CRM answers everyday questions like:

- Who are our customers, and how do we reach them?
- What deals are we working, and what are they worth?
- What did we last say to this person, and what happens next?

Salesforce is the world's most widely used CRM, and CRM Analytics sits directly on top of its data.

## A short history of CRM

Understanding how CRM evolved makes today's concepts click into place:

- **1950s–80s — paper and Rolodex.** Salespeople tracked contacts on physical cards and in notebooks. Knowledge lived in individual heads.
- **1980s — contact management software.** The first digital address books (ACT!, Goldmine) moved contacts onto computers.
- **1990s — "CRM" is coined.** Tools added pipelines, forecasting, and support tracking; the term *Customer Relationship Management* took hold.
- **1999 — CRM moves to the cloud.** Salesforce launched CRM you access in a browser, with no software to install — the "No Software" era.
- **2010s — the platform era.** CRM became a platform: custom objects, automation, app ecosystems, and mobile.
- **2014 onward — analytics & AI.** Wave/Einstein Analytics (now **CRM Analytics**) and Einstein AI brought large-scale exploration and prediction directly onto CRM data.

::note
Each leap added more data. Today a CRM holds far more than a team can read by hand — which is exactly the problem analytics exists to solve.
::

## Core CRM objects

Salesforce organizes data into **objects** (think: tables). A handful appear in almost every analytics project:

- **Lead** — an unqualified prospect you haven't fully vetted yet.
- **Account** — a company or organization you do business with.
- **Contact** — a person, usually linked to an account.
- **Opportunity** — a potential deal, with an amount, a stage, and a close date.
- **Case** — a customer support or service request.
- **Activity** — tasks and events (calls, emails, meetings).

::tip
When you load `"Opportunities"` in a SAQL query later in the course, *this* is the object you are querying. Knowing these objects makes every dataset self-explanatory.
::

## The sales pipeline

The **pipeline** is the heart of CRM reporting, so it's worth understanding early. An opportunity moves through ordered **stages**:

1. **Prospecting** — early interest, little commitment.
2. **Qualification** — confirmed need, budget, and fit.
3. **Proposal** — a formal offer is on the table.
4. **Negotiation** — terms are being agreed.
5. **Closed Won** or **Closed Lost** — the deal is decided.

Each stage carries an **amount** and a probability, so summing amounts by stage tells you the health of future revenue — the single most common analytics question you'll answer.

## Why CRM data needs analytics

A growing CRM quickly outpaces native reports:

- Data spans **many objects** (opportunities, accounts, activities) that must be blended.
- Volumes reach **millions of rows**, too large for spreadsheets.
- Leaders want **trends, forecasts, and "what should we do"** — not just lists.

That gap is the entire reason CRM Analytics exists. With the vocabulary from this lesson — objects, the pipeline, stages, amounts — you're ready to meet the platform itself.
